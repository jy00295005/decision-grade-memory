# 4. Evaluation Design and Preliminary Pilot

## 4.1 Evaluation objective and scope

This chapter evaluates a narrow empirical question derived from the paper's central method claim. The method section proposed a scenario memory layer as an organizing abstraction for turning distributed graph-based SME context into explicit decision episodes for decision-grade context construction. The evaluation objective is therefore not to establish general business value, production readiness, or broad superiority of graph-based systems. It is to test whether a more scenario-oriented context construction strategy can yield more decision-grade context than a more fragment-oriented retrieval strategy on SME-style decision tasks when both conditions operate over the same underlying business graph.

The focus on context quality is deliberate. The literature review suggested that SME decision support is often constrained by fragmented information, limited analytical capacity, and uneven visibility into goals, constraints, actions, and outcomes. Section 3 addressed that problem conceptually by arguing that retrieval alone may surface useful fragments without recovering the decision situation in a form suitable for reasoning. This chapter takes the smallest defensible empirical slice of that argument. It asks whether context packages that are assembled in a more scenario-oriented way are more complete, more traceable, and more useful for bounded SME decisions than context packages assembled through fragment-oriented retrieval alone.

The chapter is also explicit about non-claims. It does not test universal business effectiveness, causal impact on firm performance, or the final validity of a fully implemented scenario memory system. The current study is better understood as an evaluation design plus a preliminary pilot. Its role is to determine whether the comparison logic is viable and whether the early evidence is promising enough to justify a larger next-round experiment.

## 4.2 Tasks, cases, and evaluation units

The experiment is organized around SME-style decision situations rather than around generic question answering. A decision situation is defined here as a bounded evaluation case containing a natural-language decision query, a focal business problem, a shared graph-based information source, and two alternative context construction conditions. This choice reflects the current state of the implementation. Section 3 introduced the decision episode as the conceptual unit of scenario memory, but the current graph does not yet expose a clean explicit `DecisionEpisode` object that can be used directly as the evaluation unit. The practical unit for this chapter is therefore the decision situation.

The selected tasks are intentionally short-horizon and business-specific. They were chosen because the existing graph already contains problem traces, recommendation or action traces, and at least partial goal or outcome traces for them. The current pilot centers on three tasks: supplier minimum-order-quantity pressure under cash-flow constraints, weekend staffing conflict under service constraints, and sales decline recovery under attribution uncertainty. These tasks are appropriate because they are weakly structured, require more than isolated facts, and depend on putting together several contextual elements at once: a problem, a constraint, a candidate response, and some indication of target state or consequence.

Case selection should remain conservative. A usable case must be decision-oriented, clearly SME-relevant, and grounded in enough graph material to support a real comparison between conditions. In practice, this means the graph should expose at least one challenge or crisis trace, at least one recommendation or action trace, and at least one goal, benefit statement, or partial outcome trace. Cases dominated by unrelated retrieval, generic owner metadata, or missing action paths should be excluded from the next round. On that basis, the present chapter treats the current three-case run as a pilot and frames a `15-30` case study as the appropriate next experiment scale.

## 4.3 Conditions and same-source comparison logic

The experimental comparison is intentionally narrow. It does not compare different underlying knowledge sources, and it does not benchmark different product systems. Instead, it compares two context construction strategies over the same graph snapshot, for the same task, and with the same query intent. This same-information-source comparison logic is important because it aligns directly with the paper's claim. The question is not whether one system has more information. The question is whether one organizational strategy makes the same information more decision-useful.

The fragment-oriented baseline uses `node_rrf`. Under this condition, the context package is effectively the top matching set of nodes returned by retrieval. This is a realistic baseline because it approximates the common pattern in which relevant fragments are surfaced without stronger organization into a bounded decision structure. Its expected weakness is that it often returns useful pieces without making the relationship among problem, constraint, action, and outcome sufficiently explicit.

The scenario-oriented condition uses `combined_rrf` as a proxy for scenario-style context assembly. In the current implementation, this condition combines top nodes with richer relation facts and episode-linked traces. It is not equivalent to a fully implemented scenario memory layer, and it should not be described as such. Its role in this chapter is narrower: it approximates what happens when context is assembled in a more relational, bounded, and decision-oriented way rather than as isolated fragments. During the feasibility stage, `edge_episode` was also tested, but it proved noisier than `combined_rrf` in the inspected cases. It is therefore better treated as exploratory evidence rather than as the main comparison condition.

What differs across conditions is the assembly logic of the context package. What must remain constant is the user context, the case definition, the graph source, the query intent, and the retrieval limit unless there is a clear reason to change it. This distinction helps keep the experiment focused on context construction quality rather than broad answer quality in a product sense.

## 4.4 Metrics and scoring logic

The scoring framework focuses on four primary metrics: context completeness, evidence traceability, missing-information awareness, and decision usefulness. These metrics were chosen because they correspond closely to the paper's argument about decision-grade context. The central issue is not simply whether the retrieved material is relevant. The issue is whether the resulting context package exposes enough of the decision situation to support bounded reasoning.

Context completeness asks whether the package exposes the core elements of the situation: the focal problem, the relevant goal or target state, the operative constraint, the candidate action, and any observable outcome or partial consequence when such evidence exists. Evidence traceability asks whether the reader can see why a recommendation or action is being surfaced, ideally through relation facts, episode-linked traces, or visible links between recommendations and surrounding business conditions. Missing-information awareness asks whether the package leaves important unknowns visible rather than implying false completeness. Decision usefulness asks whether the package would provide a stronger basis for a business decision discussion than the comparison condition.

The current rubric uses a simple `1-5` scale for each metric, where `1` indicates a very weak package, `3` indicates a usable but limited package, and `5` indicates a very strong package that is clearly superior for the task. In the current pilot, scoring is based on single-reviewer human judgment. This is acceptable for a small feasibility-oriented pilot because the construct of interest is contextual quality rather than a simple surface metric. However, the limits of that choice need to be kept explicit. The pilot is unblinded, the case count is small, and some metric overlap remains, especially between traceability and decision usefulness. A stronger next-round study should therefore use a more fixed scoring sheet and introduce a second reviewer for at least a meaningful subset of cases.

## 4.5 Preliminary pilot and next experiment

The current pilot compared the fragment-oriented baseline and the scenario-oriented proxy condition on three decision situations. Across these cases, the fragment baseline generally returned relevant nodes but little or no explicit relational evidence. By contrast, the scenario-oriented condition more often exposed challenge-action-goal-outcome structure or other relational traces that made the context package more interpretable as a bounded business situation. In the pilot scoring, the scenario-oriented packages were consistently stronger on context completeness and evidence traceability. These gains were visible in the retrieved material itself rather than inferred from downstream business claims.

At the same time, the pilot should not be overstated. The data are synthetic or semi-simulated, relation quality is uneven, and many retrieved edges remain semantically weak. In several cases, the scenario-oriented package improved structure but still included noisy or weakly related facts. The pilot therefore supports only a narrow statement: under the current graph setup, a more scenario-oriented context package appears more promising than a fragment-only baseline for some SME-style decision tasks. It does not support a general claim about business effectiveness or a definitive claim about the final scenario memory framework.

The next experiment should be designed around `15-30` decision situations, ideally grouped into repeated task families such as cash and inventory pressure, staffing and scheduling, supplier response, sales recovery, and return-cost or margin-protection decisions. Queries, retrieval settings, and scoring procedures should be fixed in advance. A second reviewer should score at least part of the dataset. Most importantly, the scenario-oriented condition should be tightened so that it is less of a retrieval proxy and more explicitly centered on bounded episode-level context assembly. If that next round shows repeated gains in completeness and traceability across several task families, the evidence would count as promising. If the gains disappear once noisy cases are removed, or if the richer condition merely returns more material without improving structure, the evidence should still be treated as inconclusive.
