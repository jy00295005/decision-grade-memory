# 3. Method: A Scenario Memory Framework for Decision-Grade SME Context

## 3.1 Method objective and organizing abstraction

This section addresses a representation problem in SME decision support: how to move from fragment retrieval to decision-grade context construction. Prior sections argued that SME decisions are often made under uncertainty, resource constraints, uneven information quality, and dynamic operating conditions. In such settings, relevant information is rarely contained in a single source. Instead, it is distributed across business records, external signals, prior interactions, and evolving operating conditions. Retrieval can surface useful fragments from that distributed context, but fragment access is not equivalent to decision support. Decision support requires a bounded representation of the business situation in which goals, constraints, triggers, actions, outcomes, and evidence are organized in a form suitable for reasoning.

We therefore propose a scenario memory layer that organizes distributed graph-based SME context into explicit decision episodes for decision-grade context construction. The point is not to replace retrieval, but to make retrieval subordinate to a more suitable unit of organization. In this framing, graph-based business memory remains important because it can preserve entities, relations, temporal structure, and evidence traces across heterogeneous inputs. Yet graph memory alone is not sufficient if the decision situation remains implicit and must still be reconstructed by the user or by the reasoning layer on demand.

The term *scenario memory* is used here as a design term rather than as a standardized category in the literature. It denotes an organizing abstraction centered on bounded decision situations. This distinguishes it from document memory, which is organized around source texts; from conversational memory, which is organized around turns or threads; and from persistent user memory, which is organized around relatively stable facts or preferences. The narrower claim of this section is that these memory forms often leave episode structure under-specified when the task is decision support rather than general assistance.

## 3.2 Decision episode representation

The minimum representational unit of the framework is the *decision episode*. We define a decision episode as a bounded business situation in which an SME actor or firm pursues a goal under identifiable constraints, encounters a trigger or challenge, takes or considers an action, and interprets outcomes with respect to supporting evidence and temporal context. This definition is intentionally compact: it is not a full ontology of business activity, but the smallest representation that can support decision-grade context construction.

Conceptually, a decision episode may be expressed as `E = (A, G, C, T, X, O, V, tau)`, where `A` denotes actor or business context, `G` the focal goal, `C` operative constraints, `T` a trigger or challenge, `X` an action or decision, `O` an observed or expected outcome, `V` an evidence bundle, and `tau` temporal placement. The purpose of this formulation is not formalism for its own sake. Its purpose is to specify what must be assembled if the system is to move beyond loosely related fragments toward an interpretable decision context.

The framework relies on four core conceptual components. `DecisionEpisode` is the organizing unit that binds the situation into a coherent context for reasoning. `Constraint` makes explicit the operating limits and pressures that shape feasible action, which is especially important in SMEs given the literature on liquidity pressure, staffing limits, uncertainty, and weakly structured decision conditions. `Outcome` captures what followed, what is expected to follow, or what remains unresolved after an action or decision; without it, the framework would reconstruct problem state but not consequence structure. `EvidenceBundle` captures the supporting basis for representing an episode in a particular way and thereby distinguishes evidence-backed context construction from unsupported summarization.

These components play different roles and should not be conflated. Constraints describe conditions of action, outcomes describe consequences, and evidence supports interpretation. Optional extensions such as scenario tags or analogical links across episodes may later be useful, but they are not required for the core methodological claim advanced here.

## 3.3 Knowledge graph as the business memory substrate

The first layer of the framework is the knowledge graph, which serves as the durable business memory substrate. Its role is to store the underlying elements of SME context in a form that preserves structure rather than to decide in advance which elements belong to one decision situation. In practical terms, the graph stores business entities, relations, signals, state descriptions, temporal traces, and provenance-bearing links across heterogeneous inputs such as internal records, external market signals, conversational traces, and recommendation artifacts. This matters because SME decision settings are rarely contained in one document or one interaction. The relevant material is distributed across partial observations, changing conditions, prior actions, and uneven evidence.

The graph therefore operates as a fact and relation layer. It is well suited to representing that a firm is facing a challenge, that a recommendation addresses a problem, that a goal was partially achieved, or that a signal emerged during a particular period. It is less well suited, on its own, to expressing which subset of those linked elements should count as the bounded context for a specific decision. A graph can preserve many relevant links while still leaving the decision situation implicit. For the purposes of this paper, that limitation is central. The problem is not the absence of connected memory, but the absence of an explicit unit that organizes connected memory around a decision.

## 3.4 DecisionEpisode as an episode-level organizational layer

The second layer is the `DecisionEpisode`. This is the main organizing abstraction introduced by the method. A `DecisionEpisode` is not the knowledge graph itself, and it is not a synonym for a generic subgraph. It is an episode-level organizational layer derived from graph-based business memory. Its purpose is to bind distributed graph elements into a bounded business decision situation.

The distinction is important. The knowledge graph stores what is known about the business world in distributed form. A `DecisionEpisode` stores what should count as one coherent decision situation within that world. It therefore sits conceptually above the graph and below downstream reasoning. In the present framework, the `DecisionEpisode` is the unit that makes explicit what decision is at stake, what triggered it, what goals and constraints define it, what actions are being considered or taken, what outcomes are observed or expected, and what evidence supports those interpretations.

Accordingly, a `DecisionEpisode` should store the core situation slots rather than the full set of underlying facts. At minimum, this includes a decision focus, a trigger or challenge, one or more goals, operative constraints, candidate or enacted actions, and an evidence bundle linking these elements back to graph material. Where available, it may also include outcomes, unresolved uncertainties, temporal bounds, and a current status such as active, partial, or resolved. These elements are not intended as a maximal ontology. They are the minimum structure required to turn a distributed graph memory into an interpretable decision context.

This layer should therefore be understood as organizational rather than merely descriptive. It is the place where the method asserts that certain graph elements belong together because they jointly define one decision situation. The contribution is not to duplicate the graph at a higher level, but to impose an episode boundary that the graph alone does not provide. Whether a `DecisionEpisode` is persisted explicitly as a graph object or constructed from graph material at query time is an implementation question. Methodologically, the important point is that decision-grade context requires an episode-level unit of organization that is distinct from the underlying graph.

## 3.5 Decision subgraph as the task-bounded reasoning view

The third layer is the decision subgraph. This layer is related to the `DecisionEpisode`, but it should not be conflated with it. A `DecisionEpisode` defines the bounded business situation. A decision subgraph is the task-bounded working view used for a specific query, comparison, or reasoning step. It is therefore narrower and more situational than the `DecisionEpisode` itself.

In principle, a `DecisionEpisode` may contain more structure than is necessary for one decision query. Some of its elements may be background context, some may be historically relevant but not currently decisive, and some may be too detailed for the current reasoning budget. The decision subgraph is the query-time projection that selects the subset of episode-linked material needed for the present task. In most cases, it will be built primarily from one focal `DecisionEpisode`, though adjacent context may be added when the decision requires comparison, clarification, or evidence from closely related episodes.

This distinction clarifies the role of bounded context construction. The decision subgraph is not intended to store long-term business memory, and it is not intended to define the decision situation from scratch. Instead, it operationalizes the method by turning episode-level organization into a compact structure for reasoning. Its purpose is to present the focal goal, salient constraints, trigger conditions, candidate actions, outcomes, and supporting evidence in a form that can be interpreted together rather than retrieved as isolated fragments.

## 3.6 Layered framework and role of the LLM

The full framework can therefore be described as a layered path from memory to reasoning: knowledge graph, `DecisionEpisode`, decision subgraph, and LLM reasoning layer. These layers answer different questions. The graph answers what business entities, states, signals, and relations are present. The `DecisionEpisode` answers which of those elements belong to one bounded decision situation. The decision subgraph answers which part of that episode structure should be exposed for the current reasoning task.

This layered view clarifies the paper's claim. The contribution is not “graph instead of LLM,” nor “memory instead of retrieval.” It is a scenario-oriented organization of graph-based business memory that makes explicit decision episodes available for bounded context construction. Retrieval remains relevant, but its role changes. It becomes one component in assembling an episode-aware reasoning view rather than the endpoint of the support process.

The LLM is therefore positioned as a reasoning layer over structured memory rather than as the sole repository of business context. Its role is to operate on prepared scenario-aware context: explaining the episode, comparing alternatives, summarizing the relation among goals, constraints, actions, and outcomes, and supporting judgment. This positioning is methodologically important because it keeps the section focused on context organization rather than on generation alone. The framework does not assume that language models are incapable of useful memory behavior; it argues more narrowly that decision support benefits when the reasoning layer receives context already organized around explicit decision episodes and then further bounded as a decision subgraph for the task at hand.

## 3.7 Scope boundaries

The method presented here is a conceptual design for scenario-oriented context organization. It is not an empirical validation claim, and it does not assume that scenario memory is already a stabilized term in the literature. Nor does it claim that the proposed design is sufficient for all SME decision domains or that it has already demonstrated superior decision performance.

The narrower and more defensible claim is that graph-based business memory is useful but not sufficient on its own when the relevant decision situation remains distributed across heterogeneous data and signals. The proposed scenario memory layer addresses that gap by organizing distributed context into explicit decision episodes, which in turn support bounded context assembly through a decision subgraph. In that sense, the contribution of this section is methodological: it specifies a minimal framework for moving from graph-based fact storage to episode-level organization and then to decision-grade context construction.
